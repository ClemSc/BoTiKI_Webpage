An open AI system for describing soil fauna communities and its application to improve the modelling of greenhouse gas emissions from soil

Soil Biodiversity and Climate

Soils are the foundation of terrestrial ecosystems. They provide essential ecosystem services such as the control of pathogens, food production, and more. Soil functioning leads to the storage or release of potent greenhouse gases (GHGs): CO₂, N₂O, and CH₄. The soil microbiome (fungi and bacteria) is considered the main driver of the production of these gases, which arise as by-products of its activity. The activity of the microbiome is regulated by the micro- and mesofauna: small invertebrates that either feed directly on microorganisms or consume dead organic matter. In the BoTiKI project, we aim to decipher the role of soil fauna and investigate its value as an indicator of GHG fluxes from soils.

AI-supported workflow

Analysing samples of soil fauna in fluid is a time consuming task. We are developing a streamlined computer-vision workflow allowing to digitize efficiently collection tubes full of specimens. We are aiming at an affordable, scalable and open-source solution, crafted to be convenient and integrated in the usual soil zoology practices.

Computer vision and DNA

Using computer vision, we count, classify and precisely estimate the biomass of tiny animals. Using high-throughput DNA approaches, we access their diversity at the genetic level. Our tube to tube pipeline makes our design flexible. A taxonomist can digitize its catches and safely recover the individuals for through inspection, an ecologist can use the results directly, the recover sample is ready for being put in collection, or can be used for processing in the molecular lab.
